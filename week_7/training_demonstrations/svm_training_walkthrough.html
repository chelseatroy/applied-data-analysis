Support Vector Machine — Training Walkthrough

From raw data to maximum-margin classifier, step by step

Steps

The Core Idea

A Support Vector Machine (SVM) is a classifier that asks a specific question: among all possible lines (or planes) that correctly separate two classes, which one is best?

The SVM's answer: the best boundary is the one that keeps the most distance from the nearest point of each class. That distance is called the margin, and the points sitting exactly on the margin lines are the support vectors.

Intuition: Imagine you have to draw a road between two neighborhoods. A narrow road is dangerous — one pothole and you drift into the wrong lane. An SVM draws the widest possible road. The curbs of that road are defined by the houses (data points) closest to the boundary.

This walkthrough uses an 8-point dataset. We'll watch the SVM find the margin, identify the support vectors, handle a non-linearly separable case, and understand what the kernel trick does.

The Dataset

Eight points, two classes. Blue = positive (+1), Red = negative (−1). They are linearly separable — a straight line can perfectly divide them.

The two classes are clearly on opposite sides. But there are infinitely many lines that separate them — which one should we pick?

Many Possible Boundaries

All three lines below correctly classify every training point. A perceptron might stop at any of them. Which is safest?

The yellow line hugs the red class — a new red point slightly left would be misclassified. The green line hugs the blue class. The purple line is roughly in the middle but not precisely centered. We need a principled way to find the optimal middle.

The Margin

The SVM draws two parallel lines — one tangent to the nearest positive point, one tangent to the nearest negative point. The decision boundary is the line exactly halfway between them. The perpendicular distance between the two margin lines is the margin width.

Margin

Maximized gap between the two classes

SVM training is the optimization problem of finding the orientation and position of this boundary that maximizes the margin width, subject to all points being on the correct side.

Support Vectors

The margin lines touch exactly the points closest to the boundary. These are the support vectors. They are the only training points that actually matter for the final model — if you deleted every other point, the SVM would produce the identical boundary.

The Optimization Problem

Let the decision boundary be defined by weight vector w and bias b: w · x + b = 0. Points on the positive margin satisfy w · x + b = +1; points on the negative margin satisfy w · x + b = −1.

Margin width = 2 / ‖w‖

Maximizing the margin is equivalent to minimizing ‖w‖². The constraints say every training point must be on the correct side of its margin line:

minimize ½‖w‖²
subject to yᵢ(w · xᵢ + b) ≥ 1 for all i

This is a convex quadratic program — it has a unique global solution. The solver uses Lagrange multipliers (dual formulation) to find it efficiently.

C parameter (soft margin): Real data is rarely perfectly separable. Adding a penalty term C·Σξᵢ allows points to violate the margin by slack ξᵢ. Large C = small margin, few violations. Small C = wide margin, more violations allowed.

Classifying New Points

Once trained, prediction is fast: compute w · x + b for the new point. If positive → class +1; if negative → class −1. The sign tells you which side of the boundary the point falls on.

The ★ gold point (4.5, 5.5) lands in the blue region — predicted +1 (Blue). The ★ orange point (5.5, 3.5) lands in the red region — predicted −1 (Red). Both are well inside the margin of their predicted class.

When Data Isn't Linearly Separable

Sometimes no straight line works. The data below has a circular class boundary — a line through the original 2D space can't separate them.

SVM's answer: lift the data into a higher-dimensional space where it does become linearly separable. For the circular data above, mapping (x₁, x₂) → (x₁, x₂, x₁² + x₂²) adds a third axis equal to the distance from the origin. In that 3D space a flat plane cleanly separates inner (red) from outer (blue) points.

The catch: Explicitly computing coordinates in a very high-dimensional space is expensive. The kernel trick avoids this.

The Kernel Trick

The SVM dual formulation only ever needs dot products between pairs of training points, not the coordinates themselves. A kernel function K(xᵢ, xⱼ) computes the dot product in the transformed space directly, without the transformation.

Choosing a kernel: Start with RBF — it handles most cases well. Linear kernel works when features are already high-dimensional (e.g. text). Polynomial and Sigmoid are less common in practice.

Why SVMs Win (and When They Don't)

Where SVMs excel:

  • High-dimensional data — text classification, genomics. The margin objective still works even when features > samples.
  • Clear margin of separation — when classes don't overlap, SVMs find the cleanest possible boundary.
  • Kernel flexibility — non-linear boundaries for free, without designing new architectures.
  • Theoretical guarantees — the VC dimension gives generalization bounds tied to the margin width.

Where SVMs struggle:

  • Large datasets — fitting the QP scales poorly with n (roughly O(n²) to O(n³)).
  • Noisy, overlapping classes — C tuning becomes critical; wrong C causes underfitting or overfitting.
  • Probability outputs — SVMs produce a decision score, not a calibrated probability (Platt scaling can help).
  • Kernel selection — RBF γ and polynomial degree must be tuned; bad choices hurt badly.
Comparison to boosting: AdaBoost and XGBoost build many weak models sequentially and combine them. SVMs build one model that finds the globally optimal margin. Both are strong on tabular data; SVMs shine on small high-dimensional datasets; boosting usually wins on larger structured datasets.
