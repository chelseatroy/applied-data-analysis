Coffee Consumption and All-Cause Mortality: 12-Year Follow-Up from a Prospective Cohort
American Journal of Epidemiology, 2018  ·  N = 48,920
Among 48,920 adults in a prospective cohort, those consuming 3–4 cups of coffee daily showed 18% lower all-cause mortality over 12-year follow-up compared to non-drinkers (HR = 0.82, 95% CI: 0.77–0.88), after adjustment for age, sex, BMI, and smoking status. The association was dose-dependent up to 4 cups/day. The inverse association held for both caffeinated and decaffeinated coffee.
Design: Prospective cohort Adjusted for: age, sex, BMI, smoking Key stat: HR = 0.82 (95% CI: 0.77–0.88)
Causation & Confounding Station 1 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Coffee drinkers cluster with a constellation of healthy behaviors — higher income, regular exercise, Mediterranean diet, historically lower smoking rates — that aren't fully disentangled by the listed adjustments. Residual confounding from unmeasured or poorly measured variables remains substantial. Smoking adjustment at a single time point doesn't capture lifetime exposure, and "BMI" doesn't capture diet quality. The exposure is correlated with the real causes through socioeconomic and cultural channels surveys can't fully untangle.

What to Do Instead

Mendelian randomization uses genetic variants that affect caffeine metabolism (e.g., CYP1A2) as instrumental variables — they cause variation in coffee intake without causing variation in other lifestyle factors. This design breaks confounding by lifestyle clustering. Short-term RCTs of coffee consumption exist; long-term mortality trials are impractical, which is part of why the question remains open.

ML Impact

Training data encodes confounded associations as ground truth. A mortality-prediction model may learn that coffee intake predicts longevity — because coffee drinkers cluster with healthy behaviors — and fail when deployed in a population where that cultural correlation doesn't hold.

Core Principle
Observational associations often measure a lifestyle cluster, not a single cause. Adjustment shifts residual confounding to unmeasured variables — it never eliminates it.
Station 2 of 19
Residential Green Space and the Rate of Cognitive Decline in Older Adults
Environmental Health Perspectives, 2022  ·  N = 11,348  ·  Ages 55+, 47 U.S. metro areas
Using satellite-derived NDVI (Normalized Difference Vegetation Index) as a measure of neighborhood greenness, we assessed cognitive function annually in 11,348 adults aged 55+. Those in the highest quartile of green space showed 24% slower cognitive decline over 10 years (β = 0.31, SE = 0.06, p < 0.001) compared to the lowest quartile, adjusting for baseline cognition, education, physical activity, and PM₂.₅ air pollution.
Exposure: Satellite NDVI Adjusted for: cognition, education, activity, PM₂.₅ Key stat: β = 0.31, p < 0.001
Causation & Confounding Station 2 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Neighborhood NDVI is a proxy for wealth. Wealthier neighborhoods have more green space, better air quality, lower crime, less chronic stress, and residents with better access to healthcare and healthier baseline habits. NDVI is measuring socioeconomic status from space as much as it's measuring nature. Physical activity and PM₂.₅ adjustments are both measured with substantial error (self-report; annual averages), which means residual confounding from these variables persists in the adjusted model.

What to Do Instead

Natural experiments — construction of new parks, urban greening programs — enable difference-in-differences designs that compare cognitive trajectories before and after green space arrives in a neighborhood. These use neighborhoods as their own controls, eliminating time-stable confounders. Within-person longitudinal models that track individuals across residential moves are even more robust to individual-level confounding.

ML Impact

Neighborhood and geographic features routinely proxy for SES in ML datasets. A model that learns a "greenness → cognitive health" signal is likely learning wealth, and interventions built from its predictions won't improve outcomes if the real driver is the socioeconomic conditions that co-occur with green space.

Core Principle
When your exposure is highly correlated with SES, no regression adjustment fully solves the problem. SES is multidimensional, poorly measured, and deeply intertwined with almost every health outcome.
Station 3 of 19
Where Should We Add Armor to Combat Aircraft? (1943)
Statistical Research Group, Columbia University  ·  WWII U.S. Army Air Forces
Engineers analyzed bullet hole locations on bomber aircraft returning from missions over Europe. The data showed damage concentrated on the wings, fuselage midsection, and tail. The initial recommendation was to add armor plating to these heavily damaged areas, as they sustained the most hits. Statistician Abraham Wald was asked to review this recommendation before implementation.
Data: Bullet hole locations on returning aircraft Sample: All aircraft that returned to base
Survivorship Bias Station 3 — Explanation
What's Wrong

The data only describe aircraft that returned. The damage pattern on surviving planes shows where a plane can be hit and still fly home — not where it's most vulnerable. The planes that never returned were presumably hit somewhere not represented in the data. Armoring the already-damaged areas solves the wrong problem: those planes survived with those hits.

What to Do Instead

Wald's insight: add armor where the returning planes were not hit — the engines and cockpit. That's where the missing planes must have been hit. The correct analysis requires reasoning about the data you cannot observe, not just the data you have. He invented the missing-data reasoning that would later become fundamental to statistics.

ML Impact

Training sets are often survivorship-biased by construction. Fraud detection models train only on detected fraud; loan default models train only on approved loans. The model learns what correlates with being selected into the training set, not what correlates with the underlying phenomenon you care about.

Core Principle
Your sample is shaped by a selection process. When that process is correlated with what you're studying, the data you can observe will systematically mislead you about the data you cannot.
Station 4 of 19
Do Active Hedge Funds Outperform Passive Index Strategies? A 15-Year Analysis
Journal of Finance, 2019  ·  N = 312 funds in major databases
Analyzing 312 hedge funds listed in two major financial databases, we find that the average fund returned 8.4% annually over the 15-year period, compared to 6.1% for a matched passive index. Funds in the top performance quartile showed persistent outperformance across rolling 3-year windows. We conclude that skilled active management adds measurable alpha net of fees.
Sample: Funds currently in two databases Period: 2004–2019 Key stat: 8.4% vs 6.1% annual return
Survivorship Bias Station 4 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Funds that perform poorly close. When they close, they disappear from most financial databases. A study of funds "currently in databases" is therefore averaging only the survivors — the funds that happened to perform well enough to stay open. Researchers estimate this inflates measured average returns by 2–4 percentage points annually, enough to make a mediocre strategy look like it generates genuine alpha.

What to Do Instead

Use survivorship-bias-free databases that track defunct funds alongside active ones (e.g., CRSP Mutual Fund Database, Morningstar with dead-fund coverage). Analyses should reconstruct the fund universe as it existed at the start of the study period and follow all funds — including those that subsequently closed — through the end.

ML Impact

NLP models trained on published text learn what gets published, not what gets written. Recommendation systems trained on purchased items miss items users saw but rejected. Any ML application where the training label is conditioned on a prior selection process will inherit survivorship bias from that process.

Core Principle
Any sample of "successful X" is missing the unsuccessful X. Average performance in a survivorship-biased sample will look much better than the average across all attempts.
Station 5 of 19
Sex Bias in Graduate Admissions: Data From Berkeley (Bickel et al., 1975)
Science, 1975  ·  N = 12,763 applicants  ·  Fall 1973 admissions cycle
UC Berkeley's 1973 graduate admissions data appeared to show clear sex bias. Men were admitted at 44%, women at 35% — a difference large enough to reach statistical significance (χ² p < 0.001). The university faced potential legal challenge. Researchers were asked to investigate whether the discrepancy reflected discriminatory admissions decisions by departments.
Men: 8,442 applied, 3,715 admitted (44%) Women: 4,321 applied, 1,512 admitted (35%)
Simpson's Paradox Station 5 — Explanation
What's Wrong

When Bickel et al. broke the data down by department, the gender gap reversed or disappeared in most departments — women were admitted at equal or higher rates than men in nearly every department individually. The aggregate comparison was confounded by department selectivity. Women disproportionately applied to highly competitive departments (humanities, medicine, law — ~25% admit rates) while men applied more to accessible departments (engineering, chemistry — ~65–80% admit rates). The apparent bias was an artifact of this composition.

What to Do Instead

Always stratify by the relevant confounder before reporting aggregate statistics. A Mantel-Haenszel test or log-linear model accounts for stratification by department. The correct analysis is the department-level analysis, not the university-wide aggregate. The paper became a classic demonstration that aggregate statistics can reverse within every subgroup.

ML Impact

Fairness audits can show Simpson's paradox: a model may appear to have equal error rates across demographic groups in aggregate while being systematically biased within every subgroup. This is why fairness must be evaluated stratified by relevant confounders — aggregate metrics alone can be actively misleading.

Core Principle
An aggregate association can reverse in every subgroup when groups are unevenly distributed across strata with different baseline rates. Always ask: what third variable might be driving this?
Station 6 of 19
Mindfulness Training Reduces Self-Reported Anxiety: A Sequential Trial
Psychological Science, 2020  ·  Final N = 64 (32 per group)
Adults were randomly assigned to 4-week mindfulness training or a wait-list control. Anxiety was measured at baseline and post-intervention. We recruited participants in waves, testing for group differences after each wave of 10 participants per group. We stopped recruitment when a significant effect emerged (p = 0.041) at n = 64. The mindfulness group showed a 12-point reduction on the GAD-7 versus 5 points in the control group (d = 0.52, 95% CI: 0.01–1.02).
Design: RCT, wave-based recruitment Stopping rule: Stop when p < 0.05 Final p: 0.041
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 6 — Explanation
What's Wrong

If you check significance after every batch of new participants and stop when p < 0.05, the actual false positive rate is far higher than 5%. Simulations show that this "optional stopping" approach produces a false positive roughly 22% of the time — even when the null hypothesis is true and procedure looks correct at each individual look. The decision to stop, made on the basis of having achieved significance, is itself a test — and it inflates the error rate.

What to Do Instead

Pre-register a fixed sample size and stopping rule before any data are collected. If sequential testing is genuinely needed (as in clinical trials where early stopping may be ethical), use alpha-spending functions — O'Brien-Fleming or Pocock bounds — that distribute the Type I error budget across planned interim looks without exceeding the overall α = 0.05.

ML Impact

Hyperparameter search and neural architecture search are implicit mass hypothesis testing. Running 50 experiments and reporting the best validation score inflates expected performance by the same mechanism as optional stopping. A truly held-out evaluation set — never used for any model selection decision — is the only valid performance estimate.

Core Principle
Significance is only valid if it was genuinely possible not to achieve it. A stopping rule based on having achieved significance guarantees you will eventually achieve it — it's a matter of time, not evidence.
Station 7 of 19
Neural Correlates of Interspecies Perspective Taking in the Post-Mortem Atlantic Salmon
Bennett et al., 2009  ·  Demonstrated at Human Brain Mapping conference
A dead Atlantic salmon was shown photographs of humans in social situations and asked to determine the emotion being experienced. fMRI scanning was performed during the task. A standard voxel-wise analysis (t-test, p < 0.001, uncorrected) revealed a cluster of active voxels in the salmon's brain and spinal column showing significant BOLD response to the social photographs. The study was submitted to and accepted at a major neuroimaging conference.
Subject: One (1) dead Atlantic salmon Voxels tested: ~8,064 Correction: None applied
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 7 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Testing ~8,000 voxels at p < 0.001 without correction, you'd expect roughly 8 false positives by chance alone. Bennett et al. did this deliberately to expose the problem: the "significant" voxels in a dead fish are a demonstration of what uncorrected mass univariate testing actually means. The study was accepted because the method was technically standard — the mistake is in the inference, not the computation.

What to Do Instead

Family-wise error rate (FWER) correction — Bonferroni: divide α by number of tests — controls the probability of any false positive. False discovery rate (FDR) correction (Benjamini-Hochberg) is less conservative: it controls the expected proportion of false positives among declared positives. Modern neuroimaging uses cluster-level inference, permutation testing, or Gaussian random field theory for whole-brain corrections.

ML Impact

Selecting features from a large set based on validation performance, then reporting accuracy with those features as evidence of their importance, is the ML analog of uncorrected mass testing. The selected features overfit to the evaluation set. Feature importance methods (SHAP, permutation importance) must be interpreted with the same skepticism as any undisclosed multiple comparison.

Core Principle
Statistical significance means nothing if you don't account for how many hypotheses you tested. The more chances you take, the more likely you are to find something significant by chance alone.
Station 8 of 19
Economic Threat Predicts Implicit Racial Bias: A Survey Study
Social Psychological and Personality Science, 2021  ·  N = 412
Adults completed an economic anxiety scale, an implicit association test (IAT), and a battery of explicit prejudice measures. We found that economic threat significantly predicted implicit bias (β = 0.18, p = 0.038). Outliers (3 SD from mean) were excluded; analyses controlled for age, income, and education. The IAT was selected over the explicit measures because it showed the hypothesized relationship more clearly. A median split on the anxiety scale was used for interpretability.
Outcome: Selected after analysis Exclusions: Post-hoc outlier removal Key stat: β = 0.18, p = 0.038
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 8 — Explanation
What's Wrong

A researcher who chooses the outcome measure, exclusion criteria, and analytic approach after seeing the data has effectively run many analyses while reporting only one p-value. Gelman and Loken (2014) called this the "garden of forking paths." Each decision point multiplies the number of implicit tests without the researcher ever consciously trying to cheat — they simply made reasonable-seeming choices at each step in the direction that worked.

What to Do Instead

Pre-registration locks down the analysis plan — primary outcome, exclusion rules, covariates, transformations — before data collection. Clearly labeling results as confirmatory (valid p-value inference) vs. exploratory (hypothesis-generating, no inference) is the fundamental distinction. Multiverse analysis, which reports results across all defensible analytical choices, is an emerging transparency practice.

ML Impact

Every ML pipeline decision — architecture, loss function, data augmentation, stopping criterion — is a degree of freedom. Practitioners who tune these choices while watching validation curves are running many implicit experiments while reporting one result. This is a primary reason benchmark performance in ML papers fails to replicate when others run the "same" model.

Core Principle
A researcher who makes analysis decisions based on the data is not running one test — they are running many, while reporting it as if they ran one.
Station 9 of 19
Neural Correlates of Conscientiousness: A Whole-Brain fMRI Investigation
NeuroImage, 2020  ·  N = 52 adults  ·  Domain: Cognitive Neuroscience
Resting-state fMRI data were acquired from 52 adults and correlated with conscientiousness scores (NEO-PI-R) across the whole brain. Regions showing significant correlation (p < 0.001, uncorrected) were identified, yielding 7 ROIs. Signal was extracted from these regions and averaged to create a composite neural index of conscientiousness. The composite showed a mean correlation of r = 0.71 with conscientiousness scores. We propose this composite as a potential neural biomarker for the trait.
Step 1: Find ROIs correlated with score Step 2: Report ROI–score correlation Key stat: r = 0.71
Circular Analysis Station 9 — Explanation
What's Wrong

The ROIs were selected precisely because they correlated with the conscientiousness score. Reporting the correlation between those same ROIs and the same score is mathematically circular — the r = 0.71 is inflated by construction, not by evidence. Vul et al. (2009) called this "voodoo correlations" and demonstrated that it was widespread in social neuroscience, producing implausibly high brain–behavior correlations across dozens of published papers.

What to Do Instead

ROI selection and effect estimation must use independent data. In practice: use a separate "discovery" dataset for ROI selection and a held-out "confirmation" dataset for reporting the correlation; or use leave-one-out cross-validation, which estimates the ROI–score correlation on data not used to select the ROI. The corrected correlation is typically much smaller — 0.3–0.4 rather than 0.7+.

ML Impact

Selecting top-k features by correlation with the outcome, then reporting cross-validated accuracy, is circular if feature selection used the same samples as cross-validation. Feature selection must happen inside the CV loop — applied only to training folds, never touching the validation fold — or inflated performance estimates are guaranteed.

Core Principle
When you select on a criterion, the apparent association between your selected variable and that criterion is always an overestimate. Independent data are required for an honest estimate.

Predicting 30-Day Hospital Readmission Using Gradient Boosting and EHR Feature Selection
Journal of the American Medical Informatics Association, 2021  ·  N = 22,415 admissions
From an EHR dataset of 22,415 admissions, 312 candidate features were extracted. Features were ranked by point-biserial correlation with the 30-day readmission outcome across all available cases; the top 50 were retained. We then performed an 80/20 train-test split and trained a gradient boosting classifier on the training set. The model achieved AUC = 0.87 on the held-out test set, substantially outperforming the LACE+ clinical risk score (AUC = 0.68).
Feature selection: All 22,415 cases (incl. test) Split: 80/20 train-test Reported AUC: 0.87
Circular Analysis Station 10 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Feature selection used correlation with readmission across all 22,415 cases — including the test cases. The model has effectively "seen" the test labels during feature selection, before training. This means the test set is no longer independent of the model development process. AUC = 0.87 is an optimistic estimate of generalization, not a valid one. This is the most common reason clinical ML models fail when deployed on new patients.

What to Do Instead

All preprocessing — feature selection, imputation, scaling, encoding — must happen inside the cross-validation loop, using only training data. The correct procedure: split data first (or define cross-validation folds), then perform feature selection and model training entirely within the training fold, then evaluate on the untouched held-out fold. Only touch the test set once, at the very end.

ML Impact

This station describes an ML error directly. Data leakage also enters through: preprocessing (scalers, imputers) fit on the full dataset; time-series splits that allow future data to predict the past; and target encoding that leaks label information into features. It's the most cited reason clinical ML models achieve high published AUC but fail when deployed on real patients.

Core Principle
The test set is only a valid estimate of generalization if it was never touched during model development — not even for preprocessing or feature selection.
Station 11 of 19
Systolic Blood Pressure — Same Data, Two Guidelines
N = 1,000 adults  ·  Ages 40–75  ·  Primary care cohort
The same blood pressure readings yield very different prevalence estimates depending on which clinical guideline is applied. JNC 7 (2003) defines hypertension as systolic BP ≥ 140 mmHg. ACC/AHA (2017) lowered the threshold to ≥ 130 mmHg. In this cohort, 32% are "hypertensive" under JNC 7, but 53% under ACC/AHA 2017 — 210 additional patients receiving the same diagnosis, label, and treatment recommendation from the same readings.
Dichotomization Station 11 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Dichotomizing erases the information that 138 mmHg and 108 mmHg are treated identically, as are 142 mmHg and 185 mmHg. Cardiovascular risk is actually continuous and approximately linear across this range — a 10-point increase in systolic BP is roughly the same increase in risk whether you start at 120 or 150. The threshold is partly a clinical and political decision: the ACC/AHA change was driven partly by medication guidelines and partly by pharmaceutical industry dynamics, not a sharp discontinuity in risk at 130.

What to Do Instead

Use the continuous BP value directly in regression models predicting outcomes. Report results across multiple clinically-relevant thresholds, or better, report the full dose-response curve. If a clinical decision rule requires a binary threshold, make the threshold choice explicit and its effect on prevalence transparent — as this chart does.

ML Impact

ML practitioners routinely dichotomize continuous outcomes to frame problems as classification. A 30-day readmission classifier treats day-29 identically to day-1, and day-31 identically to day-365. The model optimizes the arbitrary threshold, not the underlying clinical reality — and threshold choice at deployment time can silently reverse which patients get flagged.

Core Principle
When the underlying relationship is continuous, a binary variable throws away information. Threshold choice can be manipulated to make the same data tell very different stories.
Station 12 of 19
Atrazine Exposure Elevates Cortisol in Larval Zebrafish: A Controlled Tank Study
Aquatic Toxicology, 2020  ·  N = 10 fish  ·  2 tanks
Larval zebrafish were divided between two 10-liter tanks: Tank A received clean water (control); Tank B received water treated with 100 ppb atrazine (herbicide). After 72 hours, cortisol was measured in five fish from each tank. Fish in Tank B showed significantly elevated cortisol compared to controls (t(8) = 3.47, p = 0.008), using a two-sample t-test with df = 8 (five fish per group). Authors conclude that atrazine causes acute cortisol stress responses in larval fish.
Treatment assignment: Tank-level (1 tank per condition) Measurements: 5 fish per tank df used: 8
Pseudoreplication Station 12 — Explanation
What's Wrong

The experiment has one tank per treatment. The five fish within each tank share water chemistry, temperature, microbiome, feeding schedule, dissolved oxygen, and any tank-specific random effects. They are not independent observations — they are five measurements from the same experimental unit. Using df = 8 inflates the degrees of freedom by a factor of 5. The true degrees of freedom is 0 (one tank per condition; you cannot estimate variance with a single observation per group). The p = 0.008 is meaningless.

What to Do Instead

The unit of replication must match the unit of treatment assignment. With tank as the unit, you need multiple tanks per treatment (minimum 3, ideally 5+). Analyze at the tank level (compute tank means, compare those) for a simple approach. Mixed-effects models with tank as a random effect are more powerful and correctly partition within-tank and between-tank variance. More fish per tank does not help — you need more tanks.

ML Impact

Batch effects are pseudoreplication in ML: images from the same scanner, lab, or hospital share technical variance that naive random splits treat as independent signal. A dataset of 50,000 scans where one hospital contributed 40,000 has far less effective diversity than it appears. Evaluation must be stratified by source — or models will appear to generalize when they're actually memorizing batch identity.

Core Principle
The unit of replication is the unit that was independently assigned to a treatment — not the unit that was measured. Subsampling within an experimental unit does not give you more independent replicates.
Station 13 of 19
Comparison of Treatments for Kidney Stones: Open Surgery vs. Minimally Invasive Procedure
Charig et al., BMJ, 1986  ·  N = 700 patients
Researchers compared two treatments for kidney stones: Treatment A (open surgery) and Treatment B (percutaneous nephrolithotomy, a less invasive procedure). Treatment B showed 83% overall success vs. 78% for Treatment A (χ² p < 0.001). The authors recommended Treatment B as the preferred first-line approach based on its superior overall success rate.
Treatment A: 273/350 success (78%) Treatment B: 289/350 success (83%)
Simpson's Paradox Station 13 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Surgeons preferentially assigned Treatment A (open surgery) to the harder cases — large, complex kidney stones. Treatment B was reserved mostly for small, easier-to-treat stones. Even though A outperformed B within every stone-size category, its overall rate was dragged down by its harder caseload. Reporting the aggregate comparison produces the opposite conclusion from the correct stratified analysis — and recommending B would steer every patient toward the worse treatment.

What to Do Instead

Stratify by stone size before comparing treatments. A Mantel-Haenszel test or logistic regression with stone size as a covariate gives the correct within-stratum estimate. When treatment assignment is not random — and difficult cases are selectively routed to one treatment — the aggregate comparison conflates treatment effect with case difficulty. Randomized assignment eliminates this problem by construction.

ML Impact

Treatment assignment bias in observational healthcare data maps directly onto training set composition bias in ML. A model trained on outcomes without accounting for case difficulty will learn that the treatment assigned to easy cases "works better" — not because of the treatment, but because of who received it. This is a central challenge in causal ML and uplift modeling.

Core Principle
An aggregate comparison that ignores case difficulty — or department selectivity, or stone size — can reverse in every subgroup. The aggregate isn't just imprecise; it can point in the entirely wrong direction.
Station 14 of 19
Aspirin After Heart Attack — and the Astrological Subgroup Analysis
ISIS-2 Collaborative Group, Lancet, 1988  ·  N = 17,187
ISIS-2 randomized 17,187 patients with suspected acute MI to aspirin, streptokinase, both, or neither. Aspirin reduced 5-week vascular mortality by 23% overall (OR = 0.77, p < 0.00001). The authors then ran a subgroup analysis by patients' astrological birth sign. Patients born under Gemini or Libra showed no apparent benefit from aspirin (OR ≈ 1.01), while all other signs showed clear benefit. The authors published this analysis themselves — deliberately — in the paper.
Overall aspirin effect: OR = 0.77 (p < 0.00001) Gemini & Libra: OR ≈ 1.01 (p = 0.94) Subgroups tested: 12
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 14 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Running 12 simultaneous subgroup analyses at α = 0.05 means you'd expect roughly one sign to show a non-significant result even if aspirin works identically for everyone. Gemini and Libra won that lottery. The authors knew this — they included the analysis deliberately, as a reductio ad absurdum, to show that subgroup fishing always produces something that looks meaningful if you run enough cuts. There is no plausible biological mechanism by which birth month affects aspirin's antiplatelet activity.

What to Do Instead

Pre-specify any planned subgroup analyses before the trial begins and apply corrections for multiplicity. Require a credible biological or mechanistic rationale before interpreting a subgroup difference as real. The overall trial result — aspirin reduces MI mortality by 23% — is the valid finding. Subgroup analyses in a trial of this size are hypothesis-generating at best, even when pre-specified.

ML Impact

Slicing model evaluation metrics by demographic group, feature bin, confidence decile, or geographic region is routine in ML — and every slice is a hypothesis test. When slices are chosen post-hoc based on which ones show the most interesting patterns, the resulting "disparities" are as susceptible to multiple comparisons inflation as Gemini and Libra are here. Pre-specifying evaluation slices before model training is the ML equivalent of pre-registering subgroup analyses.

Core Principle
Every subgroup cut you run is a hypothesis test. Run enough of them and something will look significant — not because it's real, but because you gave chance enough tries to win.
Station 15 of 19
Dark Chocolate Accelerates Weight Loss During a Low-Carbohydrate Diet
International Archives of Medicine, 2015  ·  N = 16
Sixteen adults were randomized to three groups: low-carbohydrate control, low-carbohydrate with 1.5 oz dark chocolate daily, or conventional diet. After 21 days, the chocolate group lost weight 10% faster than the low-carb control (p = 0.04) and additionally showed improvements in cholesterol, sleep quality, and well-being. The authors recommend inclusion of dark chocolate in weight-loss protocols.
N per group: ~5 Outcomes measured: 18 Journal: pay-to-publish, accepted in 11 days
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 15 — Explanation
What's Wrong

This was a deliberate sting. Science journalist John Bohannon designed a real trial — 5 participants per arm, 18 outcome variables — to prove how easily a p < 0.05 result could be manufactured and published. With 18 outcomes at α = 0.05, the probability of finding at least one significant result by chance is ~64%. He submitted to a predatory pay-to-publish journal that accepted the paper in 11 days with no meaningful peer review. European tabloids ran headlines within weeks.

What to Do Instead

Pre-register a single primary endpoint before collecting data. Apply Bonferroni or FDR corrections when testing multiple outcomes. Ensure adequate statistical power — for a true 10% weight-loss difference, far more than 5 participants per arm are required. And scrutinize the venue: a journal whose business model is acceptance fees cannot be relied upon for gatekeeping.

ML Impact

Hyperparameter search and architecture search reproduce this problem exactly: each configuration is an implicit hypothesis test on the same data. Without a held-out test set reserved until after all search is complete, the "best" configuration is whichever one won the noise lottery — the same logic as Bohannon's winning outcome variable.

Core Principle
Measuring 18 outcomes and reporting the one that crossed p < 0.05 is not a positive result — it is proof that your alpha level was wrong from the start.
Station 16 of 19
Bottomless Bowls: Why Visual Cues of Portion Size May Influence Intake
Wansink, Painter & North, Obesity Research, 2005  ·  Cornell Food & Brand Lab
Participants were randomly assigned to eat soup from a normal bowl or a self-refilling "bottomless" bowl that slowly replenished through hidden tubing. Bottomless-bowl participants consumed 73% more soup yet did not report greater satiation than controls (p < 0.05). This finding is part of a broader program of ~200 studies demonstrating that consumption is driven by environmental cues, not hunger.
Lab: Cornell Food & Brand Lab Published studies: ~200 Papers retracted: 13+
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 16 — Explanation
What's Wrong

In 2017, Wansink published a blog post bragging about how he handed a visiting scholar a dataset that had "failed to produce findings" and instructed her to "run every variable against every variable" until something p < 0.05 emerged. She found five publishable results. This is researcher degrees of freedom in action: when you can choose which outcomes to test, how to code variables, which participants to exclude, and when to stop collecting, you will always find something. Data re-analysis of his papers subsequently revealed impossible statistical patterns — means and standard deviations that couldn't produce the reported p-values.

What to Do Instead

Pre-registration locks in the analysis plan before data collection. The critical distinction is between confirmatory analysis (pre-specified, tests a hypothesis) and exploratory analysis (generates new hypotheses). Exploratory findings should be labeled as such and require independent replication before being treated as established — not cited as findings in the next study.

ML Impact

Iterating on a model while evaluating on the test set is Wansink's error replicated. Each time you look at test performance and adjust the model, you've consumed some of the test set's ability to give an honest estimate. The result is a model that is overfit-to-test, with reported performance that will be optimistic in deployment.

Core Principle
Researcher degrees of freedom — choices about outcomes, exclusions, and stopping — inflate false positive rates even without any intent to deceive. Lock the analysis plan before touching the data.
Station 17 of 19
Power Posing: Brief Nonverbal Displays Affect Neuroendocrine Levels and Risk Tolerance
Carney, Cuddy & Yap, Psychological Science, 2010  ·  N = 42
Forty-two participants were randomly assigned to hold either expansive "high-power" or contractive "low-power" body postures for two minutes. High-power posers showed significantly higher testosterone (20% increase, p = 0.05), lower cortisol (25% decrease, p = 0.04), and greater risk tolerance in a gambling task (p = 0.03) compared to low-power posers. The authors conclude that two minutes of posture change produces measurable hormonal and behavioral effects.
N: 42 (21 per group) DVs: testosterone, cortisol, risk-taking TED talk views: 75 million+
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 17 — Explanation
What's Wrong

With N = 42, three dependent variables, and all p-values hovering right at 0.05, this study was severely underpowered to detect reliable effects and highly likely to produce false positives. A direct replication with N = 200 found no significant effect on testosterone or cortisol. The pattern of p-values barely crossing 0.05 is a statistical signature of optional stopping — collecting data until results cross the threshold, then stopping. In 2016, co-author Dana Carney published a public statement acknowledging that the lab had used multiple analytical decision points that inflated false positive rates, and explicitly disavowed the paper.

What to Do Instead

Power analysis before data collection to determine required N. Pre-registration of primary endpoints. Stopping rules established in advance. Correction for multiple DVs. A registered replication before any large public claims. The TED talk seen by 75 million people was based on a result that required N = 200 to show was likely an artifact — a timeline problem that pre-registration and replication would have caught.

ML Impact

Model evaluation on small held-out sets produces exactly this pattern: with 40 test examples and multiple metrics (accuracy, F1, AUC, calibration), at least one will clear whatever threshold you've set by chance. Specifying which metric is primary before training — not choosing the best-performing metric post-hoc — is the pre-registration equivalent.

Core Principle
Small samples, multiple outcome measures, and flexible stopping is a formula for false positives. A p-value right at 0.05 from an underpowered study should raise suspicion, not confidence.

Effect size: Δ = 1.80 HAM-D pts Clinical threshold: 3.0 pts above placebo Data: published + unpublished FDA trials
Statistical vs. Clinical Significance Station 18 — Explanation
What's Wrong

Nothing is "wrong" with the statistics — that's the point. The drug-placebo difference is real and highly significant (p < 0.001). But a 1.8-point improvement on a 52-point scale, against a placebo response of 7.8 points, falls below what clinicians consider meaningful to patients. Statistical significance tells you the effect is distinguishable from zero; it tells you nothing about whether it matters. The paper's contribution was showing that decades of published trials had conflated "statistically real" with "clinically useful," in part because the unpublished negative trials had never been included in meta-analyses.

What to Do Instead

Report effect sizes alongside p-values, and contextualize them against domain-specific thresholds. In medicine, the Number Needed to Treat (NNT) and the Minimum Clinically Important Difference (MCID) are the right yardsticks. With sufficiently large samples, trivially small effects become statistically significant. Kirsch's meta-analysis also illustrated the file-drawer problem: including unpublished trials changed the overall picture entirely.

ML Impact

With large datasets, nearly any model improvement will be statistically significant. A recommendation system with 10M users that improves click-through rate by 0.01 percentage points has a highly significant A/B test — but users can't perceive the difference. Reporting accuracy uplift without asking whether the improvement clears whatever threshold makes the system worth deploying is this error in ML form.

Core Principle
Statistical significance says an effect is real. Practical significance says it matters. Large samples can render irrelevant differences "significant." Always ask: meaningful compared to what?

Female Hurricanes Are Deadlier Than Male Hurricanes
Jung et al., PNAS, 2014  ·  N = 94 Atlantic hurricanes (1950–2012)
Analyzing 94 Atlantic hurricanes from 1950 to 2012, we find that female-named hurricanes caused significantly more deaths than male-named ones (IRR = 1.47, 95% CI: 1.02–2.12, p = 0.03), controlling for minimum pressure, year, and property damage. A laboratory study confirmed that participants rated female-named hypothetical storms as less threatening, supporting the interpretation that feminine storm names reduce perceived danger and therefore evacuation compliance.
N: 94 storms over 62 years Controlled for: pressure, year, damage Effect driven by: 2 outlier storms
P-Hacking & Multiple Testing Station 19 — Explanation
What's Wrong

The entire effect is driven by two outliers — Hurricane Katrina (1,800 deaths, 2005) and Hurricane Audrey (416 deaths, 1957) — both female names. Crucially, before 1979 all Atlantic hurricanes had female names, meaning the pre-1979 portion of the sample is not a fair comparison: it contains all the deadliest historical storms in an era with less evacuation infrastructure, and every one of them is female-named. Controlling for "year" doesn't capture this structural break. Remove the two extreme outliers or analyze post-1979 storms only, and p > 0.5.

What to Do Instead

Outlier sensitivity analyses should be standard practice for small-N observational datasets: report results with and without influential observations. Era-based structural breaks require careful handling — pooling pre- and post-1979 data conflates two naming regimes. Mechanistic plausibility matters too: does any cognitive model explain how a storm's name, rather than its Category or path, changes evacuation decisions at population scale?

ML Impact

Label imbalance and historical distribution shifts create exactly this structure in ML datasets. A model trained on pooled data with an era-correlated feature will find spurious predictors. Evaluating model performance by cohort or era — not just overall — is the equivalent of removing the two outlier storms to check whether the effect is real or driven by a structural data artifact.

Core Principle
A "significant" result from 94 observations that hinges on 2 extreme outliers is not a finding — it is a sensitivity. Always report what happens when influential points are removed.
